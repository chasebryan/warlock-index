**Source base:** Allied official source tracker; official allied source

Iceland, Japan, ROK, Australia, Canada/NORAD, EU, AUKUS, ICE Pact, India/Quad,

**Analytic confidence:** High for source-family organization and corpus
cross-linking. Moderate for comparative implementation because national
budgets, legal authorities, industrial structures, audit systems, export
controls, and delivery evidence are uneven and require dated source refreshes.

**Purpose:** Provide a country-by-country source crosswalk for allied and
partner defense-industrial evidence without ranking allies, scoring readiness,
or making procurement recommendations.

**Boundary:** This crosswalk does not provide readiness rankings, procurement
advice, supplier targeting, facility vulnerability analysis, mobilization
procedures, route guidance, controlled technical-data handling, sanctions or
export-control evasion, basing exploitation, or operational planning.

## Bottom Line

The allied DIB lane is now large enough that a crosswalk is more useful than
another narrative baseline. The central rule is source separation: NATO, EU,
AUKUS, ICE Pact, Quad, and national evidence all matter, but none is
interchangeable. National strategy language must be checked against budgets,
implementation pages, contracts, parliamentary/audit evidence, and oversight
before stronger delivery claims.

## Evidence Ladder

1. Source-family identity.
2. Strategy or policy baseline.
3. Budget or legal authority.
4. Program, contract, or implementation page.
5. Oversight, audit, parliamentary, GAO/CRS/CBO, or equivalent check.
6. Crosswalk into DIB, allied matrix, and source registers.

## Information Gaps

- National implementation evidence is uneven across allies.
- Parliamentary/audit sources are essential before comparative claims.
- EU and NATO evidence should not be used as a substitute for national
  delivery, workforce, or industrial output.
- Partner lanes such as India/Quad and ASEAN/claimant-state routes need
  terminology separation from treaty-allied lanes.
- Public products must avoid readiness rankings, procurement advice, supplier
  targeting, facility vulnerability, route guidance, or operational planning.
